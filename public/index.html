Agent 01

CRM Maintenance Agent

Build this first CRM data rots at 20–30% per year. Every agent in the stack reads from this foundation. Don't build any of the others until this one is running.

This is the one nobody demos at conferences. Unlike the pipeline reactivation agent — which makes targeted, signal-driven decisions about what's worth pursuing — the CRM maintenance agent works on everything, all the time, regardless of deal status. It doesn't decide what to pursue. It decides what's accurate.

It runs continuously in the background: validating emails, flagging job changes, merging duplicates, closing deals that have gone cold by every objective measure. It's not exciting. It's not the one you put in the board deck. But it's the one you build first — because without it, every agent above it degrades over time.

The compounding problem it solves: a pipeline worked by an outbound agent is only as good as the contact data underneath it. An AI agent won't notice that the "hot prospect" it's personalising outreach for changed jobs fourteen months ago. It'll just keep going — faster, at higher volume, with worse results. The maintenance agent stops that before it starts.

Agent 02

Inbound Qualification Agent

Prerequisite Clean CRM contact data. If duplicates exist or company records are stale, the routing logic misfires — a contact gets flagged as new when they're a returning prospect, or a competitor slips through.

The inbound qualification agent fires the moment a lead hits your form. It enriches the contact through Clay and Apollo — firmographics, LinkedIn headcount, job postings, funding data — checks whether anyone from that company has been in your CRM before, scores them by fit and intent, and routes them into one of several specialist branches, each with its own follow-up logic.

For high-value ICP fits, a fully briefed Slack notification lands with the AE and a context-aware meeting request goes to the prospect simultaneously — not a generic "book a call" link, but a message that acknowledges what they submitted and gives them a direct calendar link. All of this happens in under three minutes — before your competitor has opened the notification.

Agent 03

Lead Nurturing Agent

Prerequisite Accurate contact data and a clean record of prior touchpoints. The agent needs to know what a prospect has already received — otherwise it re-sends, contradicts itself, or picks up a dead conversation as if it were live.

The lead nurturing agent doesn't run on a drip timer. It monitors genuine buyer signals — page revisits, content consumption, and social engagement on LinkedIn. For US markets, IP-reveal tools like RB2B add an additional layer of intent data. In Europe, GDPR-compliant visitor identification tools like Dealfront fill that gap — different data source, same principle.

Respond to signal, not to schedule. It knows what the prospect has looked at, what they haven't responded to, and what message is appropriate right now. The difference between this and a standard nurture sequence is the difference between a relevant conversation and noise. A prospect who revisited your pricing page twice this week and watched a product demo is not the same conversation as one who opened one email three months ago.

The agent surfaces that distinction and acts on it — the message that goes out references what they've actually engaged with, not a generic "just checking in."

Agent 04

Pipeline Reactivation Agent

Prerequisite Accurately recorded lost reasons. If the CRM shows "closed lost" with no reason, the agent can't distinguish a timing objection from a fundamental product gap — and it can't surface the ones worth waking up.

The pipeline reactivation agent does the work nobody wants to do manually. It goes through your dormant deals — not blindly by age, but intelligently. It cross-references the original closed-lost reason against current signals: stakeholder changes on LinkedIn, new hires, funding rounds, tech stack shifts.

A deal lost for timing two quarters ago, at a company that just hired a new VP Sales, is a very different conversation than one lost on missing functionality that still doesn't exist. The agent surfaces the ones genuinely worth waking up. Everything else gets permanently archived.

This is also the agent that exposes pipeline inflation most directly. When it runs its checks and finds nothing to justify reactivation, it archives the deal. Do that across your full pipeline and the number becomes visible fast: you had 80 opportunities marked "nurturing." The agent archives 55 of them. What's left is your real pipeline. That's uncomfortable — and that's the point. The outbound agent running on clean pipeline is a fundamentally different system than one running on a graveyard.

Agent 05

Outbound Signal Agent

Prerequisite All four agents above running. Clean contact data, no duplicates, accurate pipeline, a live nurture layer. The outbound agent is the most visible — and the most brittle if the foundation underneath it is wrong.

The outbound signal agent doesn't work from a static list. It monitors for real buying triggers — funding announcements, SDR hiring posts, tech stack changes, new executive hires. Each of these is a signal of intent: a company adding headcount in sales is probably buying sales tools, a new CRO is likely reviewing the existing stack, a funding round opens budget that didn't exist last quarter.

It fires personalised outreach at the exact moment of relevance — not three weeks later when the list gets refreshed. Right now, when the window is open.

Every contact routes through a classification matrix: persona, seniority, vertical, company size, geography, signal type. The agent generates three outputs per contact: a personalised email, a LinkedIn message, and a call brief — all tailored to the same signal and routing path, all delivered to the BDR for review before anything launches. The BDR doesn't research or write. They review, approve, and execute.

A VP Sales at a Series B SaaS company that just posted three SDR roles gets a completely different set of outputs than a CRO at a mid-market logistics firm that just closed a Series A. The copy isn't a variation — it's a different angle, written by a different prompt, for a different outcome. That's what the 5×5 routing matrix produces. Run the Copy Router to see it.

The future of GTM
engineering.

The five agents above operate in sequence. What comes next is agents that operate in conversation — with each other, with the CRM, with the signal layer — closing feedback loops that currently require a human in the middle.

Feedback loops

Reply data feeds back into routing.

A 40% reply rate on one persona × vertical combination is a signal that currently lives in a spreadsheet. The next version of this system routes that back into the copy agent automatically — the routing matrix updates itself based on what's actually working, without a human editing the prompt.

Agent coordination

Reactivation talks to outbound.

A deal archived by the pipeline reactivation agent because a key stakeholder left — that's a trigger for the outbound signal agent to find the replacement hire and open a fresh conversation at the right moment. Two agents, one handoff, no human in the middle. The system closes its own loop.

The CRM layer

The CRM becomes a living system.

Not a database you update manually after calls. A continuously enriched, continuously validated foundation that every agent reads from and writes back to in real time. The maintenance agent stops being a cleanup job you schedule quarterly — it becomes the heartbeat of the whole stack.

The operator role

The GTM engineer becomes the architect.

When the agents handle execution, the human's job shifts entirely to system design: which signals to monitor, how to route them, where to put the quality gates, what the feedback loops optimise for. That's where the leverage is — and it's where the discipline of GTM engineering is going.
